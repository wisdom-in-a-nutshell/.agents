# AIP Positioning Notes

Concise notes preserved from older positioning work in `adi-brand`.
Use this as reference material, not as default memory.

## Why this note exists

The current company memory already captures the main AIP positioning. This file
keeps a few useful reasoning details that are still worth looking up later.

## Best-fit customer pattern

The strongest customers shared a few traits:

- unique individual workflows for editing, thumbnails, publishing, and posting
- preference for not depending on a patchwork team of people
- very limited time
- willingness to pay to save time
- enough financial means that time, not money, is the main constraint

## Category framing tension

A recurring positioning problem was category confusion:

- calling AIP a **tool** anchors to self-serve expectations and lower pricing
- calling AIP a **traditional agency** anchors to slower, more manual service
- calling it a **production engine** is directionally right but can be too abstract

A useful external frame was closer to:

- done-for-you podcast post-production service
- AI-powered embedded production partner
- podcast operations partner

The important point is clarity first, then differentiation.

## Real competitive alternatives

The most realistic substitutes for best-fit customers were:

1. traditional podcast agencies
2. patchworks of freelancers coordinated by the client or a VA
3. in-house producer/editor hires

DIY tool stacks and "publish less" were conceptually relevant but usually less
realistic for the highest-value customers.

## Useful positioning language

A few durable framing ideas from the older work:

- Record and Relax is the core experience
- AIP adapts to existing workflows instead of forcing a new one
- retention is partly driven by workflow adaptation and style memory
- transparency and visibility matter alongside quality and speed
